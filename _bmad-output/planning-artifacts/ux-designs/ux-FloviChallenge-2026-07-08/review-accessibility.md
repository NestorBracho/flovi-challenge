# Accessibility Review — FloviChallenge

## Preamble

Scope: `EXPERIENCE.md`'s "Accessibility Floor" section plus accessibility-relevant claims in Component Patterns, State Patterns, and Interaction Primitives; `DESIGN.md`'s Colors section and color-consuming component specs (status pill, button-primary). Bar is a "consumer-grade" floor, not WCAG-AAA. Overall posture: the *intent* is good and unusually specific for a spec this size (focus trap named explicitly, aria-live named explicitly, color-only-status banned explicitly) — but two things undercut it. First, the accessibility floor asserts "WCAG AA contrast minimum on all text and status-pill text-on-tint pairs," and hand-computed contrast ratios on the actual authored token values show this is false for most of the status-pill pairings and for both secondary/tertiary text colors — the floor is aspirational, not verified against its own palette. Second, several concrete, foreseeable moments (the driver app's own realtime UI mutation, the one form in the product, motion, text scaling in a Flutter-web phone viewport) are simply absent, even though the floor otherwise reads as careful.

## Findings

- **critical** Status-pill text-on-tint contrast fails badly for `status-unbooked` (DESIGN.md lines 16-17, 126; EXPERIENCE.md line 67, 103). Computing WCAG relative luminance for `#D99A2B` text on its own `#FBEDD1` tint gives a contrast ratio of ≈2.1:1 — well under both the 4.5:1 normal-text minimum and even the lenient 3:1 UI-component/large-text minimum. This is the exact pairing DESIGN.md's status-pill spec mandates ("text = that status's full-saturation color... background = that status's tint"), and it's the *default* status shown everywhere a new request/gig is unbooked — i.e., the highest-traffic pill in the product. Gold/yellow families are notoriously low-contrast on light backgrounds; this isn't a marginal case. *Fix:* use a materially darker shade of the gold hue for pill *text* specifically (keep the current tint for background+dot), or re-run this pairing through an actual contrast checker before it ships and treat the current token as provisional.

- **critical** No stated mechanism for screen-reader users on the driver app to learn about realtime, system-driven UI mutation (EXPERIENCE.md line 85 "Booking race lost" vs. line 108 aria-live rule). The aria-live rule in the Accessibility Floor is written dispatcher-only: "a dispatcher shouldn't need to be looking at the screen to learn a request just got booked." But the driver app has its own unannounced, non-user-initiated UI change: when a booking race is lost, "Gig card shows 'No longer available' in place of the Book button for ~2s, then the card is removed from the list" — a silent visual swap-then-disappear with zero mention of an aria-live/role="status" announcement. A screen-reader user who taps "Book this gig" and loses the race gets no signal that their attempt failed, why, or that the card vanished — the single most consequential piece of feedback in the driver flow (did I get the gig or not) is delivered visually-only. *Fix:* extend the same aria-live pattern used for the dispatcher to the driver app's gig-list mutations (booking-race-lost swap and removal, and any other gig disappearing from the list without the driver's own action).

- **high** Three of the four status colors are also contrast-risky on their own tints, just less catastrophically (DESIGN.md lines 18-23, 126). Hand-computed ratios: `status-booked` (#3E7C8C) on its tint ≈3.86:1; `status-completed` (#5B8C6E) ≈3.24:1; `status-cancelled` (#B8503D) ≈3.93:1. All three clear the 3:1 UI-component floor but fail the 4.5:1 *text* floor — and status-pill text renders at label/meta scale (11.5-12.5px per DESIGN.md typography), which does not qualify for WCAG's "large text" 3:1 exception (that requires ~18.7px bold or ~24px regular). This is a systemic property of the status-pill component pattern (full-saturation color as text, on a light tint of the *same* hue, is inherently low-contrast), not a one-off token mistake. *Fix:* introduce a darker "pill-text" variant per status hue distinct from the tint-generating full-saturation swatch, and verify each against its specific tint at the pill's actual rendered font size.

- **high** `text-secondary` (#8A8074) fails the AA text-contrast minimum against both surfaces it's used on (DESIGN.md lines 11-12, 100, 108). Against `surface-canvas` (#FAF6F0) it computes to ≈3.6:1; against `surface-card` (#FFFFFF) ≈3.9:1 — both below 4.5:1, yet this is assigned to real running content: body-strong's "supporting detail," `meta` text (dates/counts/timestamps), and `label` text is allowed to use it too (line 108: "always `text-tertiary` or `text-secondary`"). This color appears on essentially every row of both apps. *Fix:* darken `text-secondary` by a few percent luminance so it clears 4.5:1 on the lighter of its two host surfaces (canvas), which will also fix it on white.

- **high** `text-tertiary` (#B0A697) fails contrast even more severely and is explicitly assigned to real content (DESIGN.md lines 13, 100). Against canvas it computes to ≈2.2:1, against white card ≈2.4:1 — both far below even the 3:1 large-text floor. Per line 100, this color is used for "timestamps, placeholder counts" — i.e., actual legible information, not decoration. At this contrast it will be near-illegible for a meaningful fraction of users with low vision, not just an edge case. *Fix:* either raise its luminance floor substantially (it will likely converge close to `text-secondary` once fixed, which may argue for collapsing to two text tiers instead of three), or restrict its use to genuinely decorative/redundant elements only (never the sole rendering of a piece of information).

- **high** No focus-ring/visible-focus token is defined anywhere in DESIGN.md, and the dispatcher app is explicitly a from-scratch build (EXPERIENCE.md line 16: "no named UI system — custom Tailwind build against `DESIGN.md` tokens"). DESIGN.md's Components section defines shadows, borders, radii, and every color role, but nothing for a focused state (no outline color/width/offset, no focus-visible treatment for buttons, chips, inputs, the modal ✕, or sidebar items). Tailwind's default reset (Preflight) strips native focus outlines without replacing them, so absent an explicit token, this is a concrete path to *zero visible focus indicator* across the entire dispatcher app — not just the modal, whose focus-trap behavior is otherwise well specified (line 106). *Fix:* add a `focus-ring` token (e.g., 2px accent-colored outline + 2px offset) to DESIGN.md's Components section and state in the Accessibility Floor that it applies to every interactive element, not only the modal.

- **high** No form-validation error handling is specified for the one form in the product — the New/Edit Request modal (EXPERIENCE.md line 68, and Accessibility Floor lines 103-108 generally). Neither Component Patterns nor the Accessibility Floor says anything about what happens on invalid/missing input (origin, destination, date are presumably required): no inline error copy, no error-to-field association (e.g. `aria-describedby`), no statement that focus moves to the first invalid field on a failed save, and no non-color error cue requirement (echoing the color-only-status rule, but for form errors, which is a separate and equally common failure mode). For a "credible accessibility floor," a form with zero specified error-handling behavior is a real gap, not a nitpick. *Fix:* add a row covering inline error text under the invalid field, an icon+text (non-color-only) error indicator, `aria-describedby` wiring, and focus-to-first-error on submit.

- **medium** No mention of `prefers-reduced-motion` anywhere, despite several specified transient/animated moments (EXPERIENCE.md lines 87, 97, 71, 85). The status pill "briefly shows `unbooked` then updates to `booked`" (line 87), the full-screen booking-confirmation interstitial appears on every successful book (line 71), and a lost-race gig card holds a "No longer available" state for ~2s before removal (line 85). Interaction Primitives (line 97) explicitly bans "hero animations on cold open" but is silent on whether the motion that *does* remain respects a reduced-motion preference. *Fix:* add one line to the Accessibility Floor: transitions/animations (pill flash, interstitial entrance, card removal) reduce to instant or opacity-only when `prefers-reduced-motion` is set.

- **medium** No mention of dynamic type / text scaling anywhere, despite the Foundation section itself flagging the driver app's unusual delivery mechanism (EXPERIENCE.md line 18): "the driver app is demoed via a Flutter **web build**... in a phone viewport." Flutter web is known to render UI to a fixed layout canvas that does not reflow with browser-level text zoom or OS dynamic-type settings the way native HTML/CSS does, and the driver layout has only 18-22px side margins (DESIGN.md line 112) to absorb any larger text before clipping or overlap. Neither file acknowledges this as a known limitation or states a supported scaling mechanism. *Fix:* at minimum, state the limitation explicitly (e.g., "browser zoom is the supported scaling path up to N%; OS-level dynamic type is not respected by the Flutter web build") so it's a documented trade-off rather than a silent gap.

- **medium** Keyboard semantics for filter chips and the search box's live-filtering are asserted only behaviorally, not as accessibility contracts (EXPERIENCE.md lines 69, 94-95). It's unstated whether chips are native buttons (Tab-per-chip, default-operable) or a roving-tabindex radiogroup (arrow-key navigation, one stop), and the search box's "live-filters the visible list; no submit step" (line 95) has no paired requirement that the changing result count be announced to screen-reader users (a text input whose associated list silently changes below it, with no `aria-live`/`role="status"` region, is a common real-world dead end for screen-reader users). *Fix:* state chips are native `<button>`s (simplest, keyboard-correct by default) and add an `aria-live="polite"` result-count region tied to the search box.

- **low** Modal focus-trap rule doesn't specify *initial* focus placement on open (EXPERIENCE.md line 106). "Traps focus; Escape closes; focus returns to the triggering element on close" is good and covers exit, but doesn't say where focus lands on entry (first field vs. heading vs. close button) — a small, standard, easily-added detail. *Fix:* one clause, e.g. "focus moves to the first field on open (create), or to the modal heading (edit)."

- **low** White-on-accent button-primary text sits right at the AA edge (DESIGN.md lines 14, 62-64). `#FFFFFF` on `#C1592A` computes to ≈4.44:1 — just under the 4.5:1 normal-text minimum, close enough that it may pass or fail depending on actual font rendering/anti-aliasing, but not a confident pass as authored. *Fix:* nudge the accent token a shade darker, or confirm button labels are bold enough at large-enough size to legitimately claim the 3:1 large-text exception.

- **low** Ambiguity over whether Notifications items use a status pill at all (DESIGN.md line 102 vs. EXPERIENCE.md line 73). DESIGN.md's Colors section claims the four status colors "must stay consistent everywhere a request's status appears (dispatcher list, driver gig card, notifications)," but the Notification-item Component Pattern (line 73) describes only a plain text line ("which request, that a driver cancelled, who was auto-reassigned") with no pill mentioned. This isn't a confirmed color-only violation, but it's an unresolved inconsistency: if a colored badge/dot is later added to notification rows without carrying the dot+text+tint redundancy the rest of the app requires, the color-only-status guarantee would quietly break at exactly the place a dispatcher is most likely to be glancing at without full attention. *Fix:* state explicitly whether Notification items render a status pill and, if so, confirm the same three-redundant-cues rule applies there too.

- **low** No skip-link or landmark/heading structure mentioned for the dispatcher web app's persistent sidebar + main-content layout (EXPERIENCE.md Foundation/IA sections generally). Minor at this stakes level, but a keyboard user re-tabbing through the same sidebar nav on every page load is a small, well-known, cheap-to-fix friction point. *Fix:* one line noting a skip-to-content link or `<main>`/`<nav>` landmark structure, if not already assumed by the underlying framework.
